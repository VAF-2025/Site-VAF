Conditions, Not Capacity: The Reality os Education in Brazil

👤 Rebeca 📅 18/04/2026 🏷️ Narrativas

I believe the struggle was never against technology itself, but against the situation I lived in. When you lack resources or conditions, everything becomes much heavier. Having to fight against the very environment where you are supposed to learn doesn't just take away the joy; it takes away your ability to see beyond your limits.

Nowadays, even with a lack of resources, studying has become more accessible thanks to technology. Even without the money to pay for courses, mentors, or private lessons, it is possible to use and learn from free content on platforms like YouTube, research websites, and educational profiles on Instagram.

The environment itself is fundamental. The difference between private and public schools is staggering. While private schools have extensive extra activities, resources to develop students, and parents with the financial means to support their children’s performance, including homes with internet, laptops, and a comfortable place to study, public schools face the opposite reality. They have a basic curriculum, extremely limited resources, and unstable family situations. Many students have no access to a computer and only own a cell phone. Furthermore, many live in small houses with four or more people, without a single quiet or comfortable spot to study.

Every day, technology advances further, and it becomes harder for people with lower incomes to keep up for many reasons: not knowing how to use it correctly, not having money for paid apps and having to use basic or free versions, having outdated or low-quality devices, or a lack of computer classes. If computer labs and informatics classes were mandatory in schools, it would lessen this social gap and eventually end the digital divide.

I experienced firsthand what it was like to live in a full house without a laptop or a computer. I did all my schoolwork on my phone, from writing texts to making slides and doing research. I used an adapter to transfer files from my phone to a flash drive so I could use them for presentations or print them out. I only truly understood this social difference when I became friends with a girl who lived in Portugal and moved to Rio de Janeiro. She had two laptops: one to use in class and another at home, along with a completely comfortable environment.

I confess that, many times, I felt frustrated by the environment I lived in, by the limitations, and the lack of resources. Having a phone with very little storage in the past for both personal use and schoolwork was exhausting. Having to uninstall apps, delete photos, and clear files every single day made me realize how heavy my situation really was.

It took a few years, but I managed to get my first laptop. And that was when I finally understood that the struggle was never against technology, but against the environment around me. I learned that it wasn't a lack of ability, but a lack of opportunity.
