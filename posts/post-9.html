One month after the tragedy: what the Juiz de Fora disaster teaches us about the urgency of climate adaptation.

👤 Raíssa 📅 19/04/2026 🏷️ Narrativas

The residents of Juiz de Fora were preparing to rest after the first day of a new week. When the first drops of rain touched the city ground on February 23, a Monday night. Everything seemed normal, just another summer rain to cool the heat. What no citizen knew, or could have predicted, was that this would be the start of the greatest natural tragedy in the city’s recent history.


By the next morning, everything had changed: dozens of homes had been destroyed by landslides, people were missing, and families had been killed by landslides. The volume of rain that fell during that night left thousands homeless and started a wave of evacuations across several parts of the city. During the following night, Tuesday, February 24, the storm continued and made the destruction even worse.


Student Luiza Caleia, 18, born in Juiz de Fora, remembers the events of those fateful nights and the days that followed: "I never imagined this would happen, the city simply woke up destroyed. It was all unpredictable. Civil defense sent alerts, but people had nowhere to go." 

Although she does not live in a high-risk area, Luiza followed the drama of the families who were displaced and had to leave everything behind. "Several hillsides washed away and many people were buried and killed. They lost everything they had."


The city of Juiz de Fora, located in the state of Minas Gerais, Brazil, is considered the 9th city in Brazil with the most residences in landslide risk areas. One out of every 4 residents in the region lives in vulnerable zones. On the nights of the 23rd and 24th, the region's terrain, combined with the absurd volume of rain, was a deciding factor in the disaster. For comparison, the expected rainfall for the entire month of February was 170 mm. However, during those dates, about 780 mm were recorded.


Although it was not possible to predict this disaster, it was caused by a phenomenon we already know: climate change. The increase in natural events is a direct result of global warming, which raises temperatures during the summer and leads to more intense rains. Projections show an even more drastic scenario: the possibility of tragedies like this repeating and hitting new regions.

Luiza, who participated in discussions during COP 30 and is an environmental activist in her community, says that the strengthening of climate change and the lack of planning in Brazilian cities are warning signs that need immediate attention: "If the amount of rain that fell in Juiz de Fora hit another city, especially considering Brazil's urban infrastructure, the same thing could have happened. It is sad that we need a tragedy to deal with the situation, but let it serve as a reflection for people about the climate adaptation of their own cities. These disasters do not wait and can knock on the door at any moment." Besides reinforcing that cities need to be prepared for these disasters, the student also explained the inequalities behind the victims' profiles and how urban planning increases these differences. "Brazilian cities are designed for the rich. If urban planning is not reviewed, environmental racism will continue. It is clear that the people most impacted are people of color who occupy vulnerable spaces."

One month after the tragedy, Luiza's words remain part of the reality in Juiz de Fora. Despite government investments and donation campaigns, thousands of families are still displaced and trying to rebuild their lives. Although the rains have stopped, the feeling of grief for all the lives and memories lost still flows through the hills and the reality of everyone who will never be the same.

As a final plea, Luiza asks that the event not be forgotten: "Think of what happened as a result of climate change and as an example that should promote change at a national level. Nowadays, we change the subject very quickly, but we cannot forget what matters." Also, recognizing the feeling of helplessness that can hit people, the young woman reinforces: "Have courage to deal with the challenges the world presents to us. Sometimes we are consumed by negative feelings and think it is not possible to walk toward the light. But it is possible! We are very strong together and we can ask for light and change."

The tragedy that hit Juiz de Fora is more than just an environmental disaster; it is a reminder to see the inequalities, urban challenges, and environmental obstacles in Brazil. More than an event to be forgotten, it is an opportunity to take control of what is to come and mobilize for the lives that can be saved. Regardless of different realities, climate change represents a predicted tragedy that approaches through intense rains, high temperatures, or rising tides. It is up to us, as a society, to recognize our fragility in search of a better future.
