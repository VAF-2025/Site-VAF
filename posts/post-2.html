Climate change is one of the main global threats of the 21st century, with repercussions that go beyond environmental issues and directly affect the global economy and trade. Changes in precipitation patterns, rising temperatures, and more intense droughts and floods have a significant impact on sectors such as agriculture, industry, and logistics. At the same time, global climate policies are influencing trade, establishing environmental barriers and creating new opportunities linked to energy transformation. Thus, the interaction between climate and trade is becoming increasingly crucial for nations seeking to be competitive and sustainable.



In terms of production, climatic conditions directly affect the supply of products for export. According to the IPCC (2022), tropical areas, including Latin America and sub-Saharan Africa, may face considerable reductions in agricultural productivity due to rising temperatures, which impacts exports of commodities essential to developing economies, such as Brazil.



A report by the FAO (2022) indicates that climate fluctuations can lead to a drastic decrease in food production, resulting in higher prices and lower competitiveness of agricultural exports. In addition, extreme weather events damage logistics infrastructure, such as ports and roads, leading to higher trade costs.


In the regulatory field, several economic blocs are linking trade and climate issues. One example is the European Union, which in 2023 launched the Regulation on Imported Deforestation, which limits the import of products related to deforestation. This legislation directly impacts Brazilian exports of soybeans, beef, and coffee, imposing demands for greater traceability in production.


Another initiative is the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM), also from the European Union, which imposes tariffs on imported products with high carbon emissions, such as steel, cement, and fertilizers. These actions highlight how climate policies are becoming instruments of trade regulation on a global scale.


On the other hand, the energy transition brings advantageous opportunities. The WTO's 2021 World Trade Report highlights the expansion of trade in sustainable technologies, such as wind turbines, solar panels, and green hydrogen. Countries that invest in innovations in this sector will be able to secure competitive advantages in the future. In the case of Brazil, the predominance of an energy matrix based on renewable sources represents an opportunity to stand out as an exporter of clean energy.


The relationship between climate and trade emphasizes the interdependence between environmental sustainability and economic growth. Climate change has become not only an environmental challenge but also a key factor for competitiveness in the international market. States that fail to adapt to new regulations and invest in sustainable production practices may be disadvantaged in global trade. On the other hand, those that manage to align environmental policies with trade strategies will have better prospects in the green economy. The future of international trade will depend on the ability to convert climate challenges into opportunities for innovation and global cooperation.
